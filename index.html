Discover Isan

Explore the hidden soul of Thailand: nature, silk, food, and traditions from the heart of the northeast.

Welcome to Isan

Isan, Thailand’s northeastern region, is a land of quiet contrast — where vibrant rice paddies stretch across flatlands and ancient ruins rise from red earth. Despite being the largest region in Thailand (covering about one-third of the country), it remains one of the least known internationally. Yet, it is rich in cultural identity, folklore, and a resilient spirit.

Isan is home to over 22 million people, with most communities engaged in agriculture, crafts, and emerging tourism. While it has long been seen as rural and underdeveloped, that view is rapidly changing as travelers, entrepreneurs, and investors discover its unique offerings.

A Brief History of Isan

Isan’s story is shaped by ancient civilizations, migration, and resilience. It shares cultural and linguistic ties with Laos, as much of its population speaks a Lao dialect and practices similar customs. Historically, the region was influenced by the Khmer Empire, and many ruins such as Phanom Rung and Prasat Hin Phimai reflect this legacy.

During the Ayutthaya and Rattanakosin periods, Isan became more integrated into Siam (Thailand), though economic and geographic barriers kept it isolated for centuries. In the 20th century, waves of migration to Bangkok and abroad brought remittances but also a sense of cultural marginalization. Today, Isan is regaining pride as it becomes more connected and celebrated for its heritage and creativity.

Arts & Crafts

Isan’s creative expression lives in its villages — in hand-dyed silks, bamboo weaving, earthenware pottery, and festivals that combine art, music, and community ritual.

Silk Weaving

Isan silk is world-renowned, particularly from provinces like Surin and Khon Kaen. Using mudmee (ikat) and natural dyeing techniques, artisans produce patterns that reflect local myths and beliefs. Each piece can take weeks to complete and is often woven at home by women using traditional wooden looms.

Bamboo Craft

Bamboo is both a symbol of resilience and a practical material in Isan. Villagers weave baskets, trays, fish traps, and even furniture. The designs blend beauty and function, often passed through generations.

Pottery

In Ban Chiang (Udon Thani), prehistoric pottery was unearthed that dates back over 5,000 years — now a UNESCO World Heritage site. Today, artisans still shape pots and ceramic wares by hand, often for cooking or temple use.

Other Crafts

Additional crafts include dyeing with indigo and herbs (Sakon Nakhon), knife-making and metalwork (Nakhon Ratchasima), handmade musical instruments like the khaen, and palm leaf weaving and lantern making for festivals.

Landscapes & Nature

Natural Parks & Wonders

Phu Kradueng National Park: A sacred mountain and hiking destination offering cliff views and waterfalls.

Sam Phan Bok: Known as the “Grand Canyon of Thailand,” a stunning riverbed with honeycomb-like rock formations.

Historical Sites

Phu Phra Bat Historical Park: A surreal stone landscape with ancient religious carvings and prehistoric dwellings.

Pha Taem National Park: Home to some of Asia’s oldest rock paintings and towering cliffs over the Mekong.

Rivers & Wetlands

Chiang Khan (Loei): A charming Mekong riverside town with colonial-era architecture and sunrise alms-giving rituals.

Red Lotus Sea (Udon Thani): A seasonal lake covered in pink lotuses — magical at sunrise.

Mekong River Provinces: Nakhon Phanom, Nong Khai, Mukdahan — riverfront towns with festivals and cross-border markets.

Cultural & Community Sites

Phu Ruea: A misty plateau known for its cool weather and pine-covered hills.

Tat Ton Waterfall (Chaiyaphum): A serene cascade surrounded by forest and picnic areas.

Kaeng Tana National Park: Forested landscapes and rapids near Ubon Ratchathani.

Hospitality: Hotels & Restaurants

Hotels

Boutique stays are on the rise, particularly in places like Loei, Ubon Ratchathani, and Nakhon Phanom. Many properties blend local materials with modern comforts, offering curated experiences such as cooking classes or textile workshops.

Restaurants

Traditional wooden eateries serve dishes from clay stoves and banana leaf wraps, but there’s also an emerging food scene. Michelin Guide Thailand has recognized several Isan establishments in cities like Khon Kaen and Ubon.

Suggested Itineraries

Visitors can follow themed trails — such as Silk & Craft Villages, Ancient Khmer Temples, or Mekong River Towns — each providing a different facet of Isan’s charm.

Flavours of Isan

Isan cuisine is rustic, punchy, and deeply local — dishes are often grilled, fermented, or pounded by hand, with sticky rice as a staple.

Som Tam

Spicy green papaya salad, in dozens of variations

Larb & Nam Tok

Chopped meat salads with mint, toasted rice, and lime

Grilled Meats

Moo ping, gai yang, sai krok Isan (fermented sausages)

Sticky Rice

Khao niew: eaten by hand, used to scoop everything

Fermented Fish

Pla ra: a key flavor base for sauces and soups

Kai Yang & Egg-Based

Grilled chicken is iconic in Isan and eggs are used creatively

Soups & Stews

Tom saep (sour/spicy soup), gang om (herb soup)

Desserts

Khao niaow ma muang (mango sticky rice), coconut treats
